About Crocuses

Crocuses come up in early spring (sometimes when the ground is still snowy!) with various vibrant colors that starkly contrast the bleak colors of winter. Crocuses are important for pollinators, being one of the few sources of nectar early in the year.

Caring for Crocuses

Crocuses will naturally spread and regrow each year, and are not bothered by common garden threats such as deer and rabbits. Crocuses prefer spots with full sun, but can still do well under partial sun. Make sure to water well after planting, and maintain soil moisture, especially in dry weather (but be careful to not over-water). Early bloomers should be protected from harsh weather with plastic coverings.

Planting Crocuses

Plant crocuses in the fall, several weeks before the ground freezes- they need to be kept in extended cold to bloom in the spring. Crocuses should be planted in early to mid fall in cold regions, and mid fall in warmer climates. Cluster bulbs together a few inches apart rather than spacing them out, and try to plant a dozen or more at a time.
